Music AI · Automatic Music Transcription · Performer-Centered Evaluation

When AI turns sound into sheet music, does it make something a musician can actually use?

This project tests a lightweight automatic music transcription pipeline built on Hugging Face Spaces. The goal is not just to generate MIDI or a score-looking image, but to ask whether the result preserves the musical information a performer needs: pitch, rhythm, meter, dynamics, voicing, and readability.

Research question

What happens when a student-accessible AI transcription tool has to produce performer-readable notation?

Annabelle’s research asks how the architectural and computational constraints of AI music transcription systems affect their ability to represent musical information — especially pitch, rhythm, dynamics, instrumentation, and notation readability.

The key distinction is simple: a model can detect notes and still fail to create a score. Musicians do not need a literal dump of sound events. They need a readable map: meter, grouping, voice separation, rhythm, phrasing, and enough abstraction to make performance possible.

Pipeline

From audio to notation

The current system starts with an audio upload, uses Basic Pitch to produce MIDI, converts MIDI into MusicXML with music21, and renders a score with LilyPond. The improved AMT Report Card adds a reference-MIDI comparison so the output can be scored rather than only described.

Tests

Three increasingly difficult musical inputs

The project uses three test cases: a C major scale, Twinkle Twinkle Little Star, and Bach’s Minuet in G. Together they show how the system behaves as the input moves from a simple monophonic baseline toward real repertoire.

C major scale

The simplest baseline. The pitch contour mostly survives, but the rhythm is wrong enough that the notation is barely performable.

  • Pitch contour loosely visible.
  • Uniform quarter notes become uneven durations.
  • This shows that rhythm fails even before polyphony becomes an issue.

Final app direction

AMT Musician Lab

The instructor demo app combines the original transcription tool and the AMT Report Card. A user uploads audio, optionally uploads a reference MIDI, and receives a musician-facing report: downloadable MIDI, MusicXML, note-event CSV, score viewer, timing metrics, pitch/rhythm scores, and a plain-language usability review.

Paper argument

Pitch detection is not musical representation.

The paper becomes stronger when it makes this distinction explicit. Basic Pitch can produce MIDI note events, but readable notation requires higher-level musical interpretation: beat, meter, phrase grouping, key, voicing, dynamics, and performer usability. The project’s contribution is to make that gap visible and measurable.

A transcription that gets some pitches right but loses rhythm, meter, and voicing is not yet a score. It is evidence that the hardest part of transcription begins after the model has detected sound events.
